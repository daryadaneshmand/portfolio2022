Snail Tracker Project

Client-facing product design

In my DSGN 384 Interdisciplinary Product Design course, I worked in a team of four on a client-based project for two quarters.

Our client, Shedd Aquarium researcher Dr. Andy Kough, studies conch movement. My three teammates and I set out to design a snail tracking device that (1) accurately and efficiently translated accelerometer data to directionality, (2) securely attached the device to the conch, (3) prevented internal movement, and (4) increased energy efficiency.

Interdisciplinary Client-Based Product Design

Research

Literature Review

Following initial client communication and identification of client & user needs, I compiled external research with my team into a spreadsheet with several key categories: Attachments, Accelerometers, Snails, Sustainability, & Other.

Our most relevant findings were included in a 14-page literature review. Client research goals and current study design were introduced, followed by outlining of relevant prior research and takeaways from other research projects tracking marine life. I primarily focused on waterproofing and on comparing alternative study designs.

Expert Sources

I connected with NOAA researcher Jennifer Doerr to learn about her team's conch tracking methodology, which informed initial directions in testing epoxy resins as an alternative attachment method, and later connected with Materials Science and Engineering Professor Emeritus Stephen Carr to better understand physical properties of such adhesives.

After team discussions with Mechanical Engineering Professor Nick Marchuk about circuits and energy capacity, I researched inertial measurement units (IMUs) and alternate arduino configurations, which were included as possible future directions in product report appendices.

Components

Process

The team identified several pain points.

  • Inconsistent attachment
    Wired attachment was inefficient, prevented material reuse, and made the device prone to shifting, which impeded data accuracy.
  • Data correction & analysis
    Accelerometer axes do not align with the heading of the conchs, primarily due to slight shifting within tracker casing (secured by a loose rubber insert and hook-and-loop velcro), variance in tracker placement due to unique shell topography.
  • Limited battery life
    1000mA battery provides power for 4 days, necessitating an additional trip to find conchs and replace batteries.

MIAMI FIELD WORK

Teammate Bryan Sanchez and I received a grant to perform observation and testing of the full user process in Miami. We began by soldering data loggers and placing them in their Go-Pro housing along with the other components. I joined our client and his partner in freediving to retrieve 16 conchs in Port Everglades. (They all look like rocks from the surface, but I managed to pull up three from the seafloor.) After placing the conchs in a saltwater mesocosm at Florida International University Biscayne Campus, we attached the trackers and tested data logger functionality. A novel alignment method was tested, and data was recorded in order to correct movement data after the testing period.

Final Deliverables

Product

  1. An improved attachment method that is optimized for efficiency, security, robustness, and ease of use.
  2. An arduino positioning cradle to secure device components.
  3. Standardized, off-the-shelf casing components that are waterproof, robust to pressure changes, low weight, easy to use, readily available, and help to easily and consistently identify each unique device.
  4. A larger capacity battery to maximize data collection time and minimize the number of trips needed to facilitate the study.

Final Product Report

The team's 42-page final product report encompassed the results of our work and justifications for key design decisions, including an executive summary, testing results and procedures, product specs, and possible future directions. I also indicated key findings from several client interview and expert source interviews in our appendices and created a summary of testing in Miami in conjunction with Bryan Sanchez.
